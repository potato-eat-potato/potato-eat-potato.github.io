Theorem

Theorem: Thomson principle

Statement

Among all unit flows \(\theta\) from \(a\) to \(z\), the energy \(\mathcal{E}(\theta) = \sum_e \theta(e)^2 r_e\) is uniquely minimized by the Unit current flow \(i\), and \[R_{\mathrm{eff}}(a,z) = \mathcal{E}(i) = \min_{\theta \text{ unit flow}} \mathcal{E}(\theta).\]

Intuition

Nature dissipates as little as possible: actual current arranges itself to waste minimal energy, so any flow I invent wastes at least as much — and my invention’s waste is a certified upper bound on resistance.

When to Use

Whenever I need \(R_{\mathrm{eff}} \le (\text{something})\): equivalently, escape probability lower bounds, transience proofs, \(G_D(x,x)\) upper bounds. This is one jaw of the variational vise; the other jaw (test potentials, lower bounds on \(R\)) is Dirichlet principle. Flows bound \(R\) from above; potentials from below.

What It Converts

A global equilibrium computation (solve Kirchhoff everywhere) into a one-sided certificate (construct anything admissible).

Proof Sketch

Decompose \(\theta = i + (\theta - i)\); the difference is a sourceless flow (cycle space); cross term vanishes because the current comes from a potential (\(\sum_e r_e\, i(e)\,\sigma(e) = 0\) for sourceless \(\sigma\) — sum by parts onto the potential); so \(\mathcal{E}(\theta) = \mathcal{E}(i) + \mathcal{E}(\theta - i) \ge \mathcal{E}(i)\). Pythagoras in disguise: flows form an affine space, energy is a quadratic form, the current is the orthogonal projection.

Typical Failure Modes

  • The candidate \(\theta\) silently violates the node law (most common bug — verify divergence at every vertex class).
  • Forgetting “unit”: flux must be exactly \(1\).
  • Infinite networks: need finite-energy flows to infinity; the principle then characterizes transience (Lyons’ criterion).
