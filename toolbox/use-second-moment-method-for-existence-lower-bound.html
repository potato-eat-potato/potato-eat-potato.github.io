Tool: Use second moment method for existence lower bound

One-Sentence Summary

If the count \(Z\) has \(\mathbb{E}[Z^2] \approx (\mathbb{E}Z)^2\), then \(Z > 0\) with decent probability — existence from two moments via Paley–Zygmund.

What Problem Does This Solve?

Lower bounds on maxima and existence statements (“some particle reaches \(vn\)”, “some site exceeds \(m_N\)”, “some structure appears in the random graph”).

Input

A counting variable \(Z \ge 0\) with computable first and second moments.

Output

\[\mathbb{P}(Z > 0) \ge \frac{(\mathbb{E}Z)^2}{\mathbb{E}[Z^2]}.\]

What It Converts

  • from: existence of an unlikely configuration
  • to: a correlation computation (\(\mathbb{E}[Z^2]\) = sum over pairs)

Core Idea

\(\mathbb{E}[Z^2] = \sum_{i,j}\mathbb{P}(X_i, X_j \text{ both high})\): split pairs by how correlated they are. The method works iff near-independent pairs dominate. For BRW, pairs = pairs of particles, correlation = common ancestry; for DGFF, correlation = \(\frac{2}{\pi}\log\frac{N}{|x-y|}\) — split by dyadic distance.

The crucial caveat: for BRW/DGFF maxima, the vanilla second moment fails near the true maximum (pairs with deep common history blow up \(\mathbb{E}[Z^2]\)). The fix is truncation: count only particles whose whole trajectory stays below a linear barrier. With the barrier, second moment matches first moment and the lower bound goes through — this is Bramson’s insight, and it’s where Use multiscale decomposition becomes unavoidable.

When Should I Think of This?

  • Immediately after a successful first moment bound, to test sharpness.
  • Whenever \(\mathbb{E}Z \to \infty\) but I cannot conclude \(Z > 0\) — the signature situation.

Typical Use Cases

  • BRW: particles near \(v^* n\) exist — with barrier truncation.
  • Thresholds in random graphs (Park seminar material).
  • DGFF: near-maximal sites exist.
