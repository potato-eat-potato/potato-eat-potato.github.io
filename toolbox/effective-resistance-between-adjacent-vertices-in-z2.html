Problem

Problem: Effective resistance between adjacent vertices in Z2

Problem Statement

Compute \(R_{\mathrm{eff}}(0, e_1)\) for the network \(\mathbb{Z}^2\) with unit conductances.

Answer: \(\tfrac{1}{2}\), exactly.

Why This Matters

Not for the number — for the demonstration: one tiny problem, three completely different tools, same answer. This is my standard test problem for any new resistance technique, and my favorite example of what this vault means by “toolbox.”

Three Solutions

  1. Superposition of currents. Inject \(1\)A at \(0\) (it spreads \(\tfrac14\) per incident edge by symmetry); separately extract \(1\)A at \(e_1\) (it draws \(\tfrac14\) per edge). Superpose: the edge \((0, e_1)\) carries \(\tfrac14 + \tfrac14 = \tfrac12\) A. Voltage drop across that unit resistor \(= \tfrac12\), and unit current flows overall, so \(R_{\mathrm{eff}} = \tfrac12\). (Unit current flow, linearity.) — but on recurrent \(\mathbb{Z}^2\) this superposes two infinite networks, which is not yet rigorous; see The rigorous version below.
  2. Potential kernel. \(\mathbb{P}_0(\tau_{e_1} < \tau_0^+) = \big(c(0) R_{\mathrm{eff}}(0,e_1)\big)^{-1}\), and via \(a(e_1) = 1\) (one-line proof in Potential kernel) the escape probability is exactly \(\tfrac12\)… giving \(R_{\mathrm{eff}} = \tfrac{1}{4 \cdot 1/2} = \tfrac12\). Bonus fact: the walk from \(0\) hits \(e_1\) before returning to \(0\) with probability exactly \(\tfrac12\) — not obvious!
  3. Foster’s theorem / transitivity. On a finite transitive-ish approximation (torus), \(\sum_{\text{edges}} R_{\mathrm{eff}}(e) = \#V - 1\); with \(2N^2\) edges and \(N^2\) vertices, edge-transitivity gives \(R_{\mathrm{eff}}(e) \to \tfrac12\). (Limit interchange to be done carefully — see below.)

The Rigorous Version (my writeup)

The superposition argument is morally right but cheats: “1A out at infinity” is undefined, and on a recurrent graph two infinite networks cannot simply be added. My write-up Nearest-Neighbor Escape Probability of SRW on \(\mathbb{Z}^2\) (PDF) makes it honest:

  1. Finite box. Work on \(B_n = [-n,n]^2 \cap \mathbb{Z}^2\) with \(\partial B_n\) wired to a single vertex. Network 1 = unit current \(o \to \partial B_n\); Network 2 = unit current \(\partial B_n \to a\); their sum is the unit current flow \(o \to a\) on the wired box.
  2. Network 1 is exact. \(B_n\) is centred at \(o\), so by symmetry the current out of \(o\) splits four ways and \(i_n^{(1)}(o,a) = \tfrac14\) for every \(n\).
  3. Network 2 needs a limit. A box centred at \(a\) gives \(\tfrac14\) too, but the two networks use boxes centred at different points; the real task is to show \(V_n^{(0)}(x) - V_n^{(a)}(x) \to 0\).
  4. Killed Green function. \(V_D(x) = \tfrac14\big(g_D(a,a) - g_D(x,a)\big)\), so the comparison is between two killed Green functions.
  5. Potential kernel closes it. With \(g_D(x,a) = \mathbb{E}_x[a(X_{\tau_{\partial D}} - a)] - a(x-a)\) and \(a(z) = \tfrac{2}{\pi}\log|z| + c_0 + O(|z|^{-2})\), the \(a(x-a)\) term cancels between the two domains and the difference \(\to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\). (Replace divergent Green function by potential kernel.)

So \(R_{\mathrm{eff}} = \tfrac12\) rests on a genuine finite-volume argument, not hand-waving about infinity — a small but honest instance of Finite volume approximation.

What I Understand

Solutions 1 and 2 completely. I wrote up the rigorous finite-box justification of Solution 1 in full (PDF, April 2026), and presented this problem in my Courant blackboard talk.

What Remains Unclear

Possible Generalizations

  • Other lattices (triangular, hexagonal): does the superposition argument survive? (Needs only vertex symmetry.)
