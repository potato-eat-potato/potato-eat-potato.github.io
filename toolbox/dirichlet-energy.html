Object: Dirichlet energy

One-Sentence Summary

\(\mathcal{E}(f) = \sum_{e=\{x,y\}} c_e\,(f(x)-f(y))^2\) — the quadratic form that secretly runs three theories at once: electrical networks, harmonic functions, and the DGFF.

Definition

For \(f\) on the vertices: \(\mathcal{E}(f) = \sum_{\text{edges } \{x,y\}} c_{xy}(f(x)-f(y))^2\). For a flow \(\theta\): \(\mathcal{E}(\theta) = \sum_e \theta(e)^2 r_e\). In my DGFF notes the polarized form is normalized as \(\mathcal{E}(f,g) = \frac{1}{4d}\sum_{x\sim y}(f(x)-f(y))(g(x)-g(y)) = \langle f, L g\rangle\) with \(L = I-P\) — the same quadratic form, carrying the \(\frac{1}{2d}\) that makes \(G = L^{-1}\) the DGFF covariance (factor warning in Discrete Laplacian).

Three Hats

  1. Electrical: energy dissipated by a current; minimized by the true voltage/current (Dirichlet principle, Thomson principle).
  2. Analytic: harmonic functions are exactly the local minimizers of \(\mathcal{E}\) given boundary values.
  3. Probabilistic: the DGFF density is \(\propto e^{-\frac{1}{4d}\sum_{x\sim y}(\varphi_x - \varphi_y)^2}\) — the field is “Gaussian noise weighted by Dirichlet energy,” so low-energy configurations are likely. Shifting the field by \(h\) costs \(e^{-\frac{1}{2}\mathcal{E}(h)}\)-ish (Cameron–Martin) — see Change of measure.

Why It Matters

Whenever I need a bound and don’t have an exact computation, the move is: guess a competitor function or flow, compute its energy. Energy turns clever guesses into rigorous inequalities — the heart of Energy minimization argument.

Why This Object Is Central to My Probability Route

It is the common currency between my Zeitouni side (resistance = minimized energy) and Bourgade side (DGFF Hamiltonian = energy). The same functional, two research programs.

Tools That Use This Object

  • Use Thomson principle by constructing flow
  • Use effective resistance to control random walk
  • Use Gaussian conditioning as Markov property (harmonic extension minimizes energy)

Common Normalizations or Conventions

Sum over edges vs. ordered pairs: factor 2. With \(\frac{1}{2d}\) or without: factor \(2d\). I record per-source conversions in reading notes; vault default is the edge sum above.

Where I Encountered This

Doyle–Snell (energy of flows), Zeitouni’s field notes (Gaussian Hamiltonian).
