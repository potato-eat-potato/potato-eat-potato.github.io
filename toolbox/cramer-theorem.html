Theorem

Theorem: Cramer theorem

Statement

(Cramér.) For i.i.d. \(X_i\) with \(\Lambda(\lambda) = \log\mathbb{E}e^{\lambda X_1} < \infty\) near \(0\), the empirical means satisfy a large deviation principle with rate $^* = $ Legendre transform of \(\Lambda\): \[\frac{1}{n}\log\mathbb{P}\big(S_n/n \ge a\big) \longrightarrow -\Lambda^*(a), \qquad a > \mathbb{E}X_1.\]

Intuition

Rare events happen in the cheapest possible way: to have mean \(a\), the walk behaves like an i.i.d. sample from the tilted law with mean \(a\), and the price is the relative entropy of that tilt — which is exactly \(\Lambda^*(a)\).

When to Use

  • Exponential decay rates for sums; speed selection for Branching random walk via \(\Lambda^*(v^*) = \log m\).
  • As the prototype: when increments aren’t i.i.d., ask “what is the Gärtner–Ellis version?” before improvising.

What It Converts

An \(n\to\infty\) asymptotic probability into a finite-dimensional convex optimization.

Proof Sketch

Upper bound: Chernoff, optimize \(\lambda\). Lower bound: tilt to mean \(a\) (Change of measure), apply the LLN in the tilted world, undo the tilt and collect \(e^{-n\Lambda^*(a)}\). The proof is itself a reusable pattern — both halves recur constantly.

Typical Failure Modes

  • Heavy tails: \(\Lambda \equiv \infty\) off \(0\); rate function degenerates (one-big-jump principle instead — different universe, don’t mix them up).
  • \(a\) outside the closure of the mean’s range: rate \(= \infty\), statements vacuous.
  • Lattice vs. non-lattice issues only matter for sharp prefactors (Bahadur–Rao), not the exponential scale.

Related Tools

  • Use Cramer transform for large deviation speed (action version)
  • Use first moment method for maximum upper bound (Cramér supplies the tail, the union bound does the rest)

Where I Have Used or Seen This

Current Understanding

Statement and both proof halves: yes. Multidimensional version: statement only.
